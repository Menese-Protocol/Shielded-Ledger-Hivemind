# Shielded ledger Phase-2 trusted-setup ceremony

This document describes the trusted-setup ceremony that produces the Groth16 proving and verifying
keys for the shielded ledger's two circuits (a 2-in/2-out join-split transfer and a deposit), over
BLS12-381. It explains how the ceremony works, the trust model, how to contribute, how to verify the
result yourself, the Phase-1 decision, the reproducible build, and the honest limits.

It is written for anyone who wants to check the parameters before trusting the ledger with value.
Nothing here requires trusting the people who ran the ceremony. It requires only that you run the
public verifier on the public transcript, and that at least one honest contributor took part.

## 1. Why a ceremony exists

Groth16 needs a structured reference string. Whoever knows the secret randomness ("toxic waste")
behind that string can forge proofs, and in a shielded pool a forged proof is silent, unbounded
inflation. A single machine that generates the keys knows that secret. A trusted-setup ceremony
removes that single point of failure by having many independent people each fold in their own secret
randomness and then destroy it. The final keys are safe if even one participant destroyed their
secret honestly. This is the "1-of-N honest" property.

The prior keys in this repository were generated by a single machine and are explicitly marked
deployment-forbidden or demo-only (see `docs/TRUSTED-SETUP-POLICY.md` and the `SETUP-MANIFEST.json`
files). This ceremony replaces that single-party setup with a real multi-party one.

## 2. How it works (apply-and-destroy)

The ceremony follows the sequential model of Bowe, Gabizon, and Miers (2017), the same shape used by
the Zcash Sapling setup, the Ethereum KZG summoning ceremony, and snarkjs `zkey contribute`.

Two phases:

- Phase 1 (powers of tau) is universal: it does not depend on our circuits. We inherit an existing,
  reviewed Phase-1 rather than run our own (see section 6).
- Phase 2 specializes the Phase-1 output to our exact two circuits. This is the ceremony this
  repository runs and coordinates.

In Phase 2, participants take turns in a queue. On your turn:

1. you download the current public parameters from the coordinator;
2. your browser samples a fresh secret from the operating system random generator;
3. your browser transforms the parameters with that secret and produces a short proof that you did
   so correctly (a proof of knowledge of the secret);
4. your browser uploads the transformed public parameters and the proof, and discards the secret;
5. the coordinator verifies your proof and appends your step to the public transcript.

The secret never leaves your browser tab. Only transformed public parameters and a public proof are
uploaded. There is no "deposit your secret" step anywhere in the design.

When the contribution window closes, a public random beacon (for example a specified future Bitcoin
block hash, or a specified drand round) is folded in as a final step. The beacon's value is
unpredictable while the ceremony is open and public afterward, so anyone can recompute it. It ensures
that even if every named participant had colluded, none of them could have predicted the final
parameters. After the beacon the parameters are frozen.

## 3. Trust model

- Security rests on "1-of-N honest": the final keys are sound if at least one contributor sampled a
  real secret and destroyed it. You can be that one. Contributing yourself is the strongest possible
  assurance for you personally.
- Public verifiability: every contribution carries a proof of knowledge bound to the running
  transcript, so contributions cannot be reordered, replayed, or forged. Anyone can replay the whole
  transcript and check every step (section 5).
- The coordinator never holds a secret. It stores only public state: the running parameters, each
  contribution's proof and parameter hashes, the participant queue and log, the window, and the
  beacon. There is no field or method that receives or reconstructs a secret.
- On-chain versus off-chain verification (measured decision). We measured the cost of verifying a
  contribution's proof of knowledge inside the coordinator. In the in-canister pure-integer
  BLS12-381 implementation this repository uses, a single proof-of-knowledge verification costs well
  over the Internet Computer per-message instruction limit; the elliptic-curve subgroup checks alone
  (each a full scalar multiplication) exceed it, before any pairing. So the coordinator runs the
  affordable structural checks on-chain when it accepts a contribution (every point is canonically
  encoded, on the curve, and not the identity; the parameters actually changed; the lengths are
  right; the running challenge chains correctly so contributions cannot be reordered or replayed),
  and records the proof and its hashes into the immutable public transcript. The soundness-critical
  subgroup and pairing verification of every proof, and the check that every one of the tens of
  thousands of parameter points was transformed consistently, are run off-chain by the standalone
  verifier over the published transcript. The standalone verifier re-runs the exact same
  proof-of-knowledge math the coordinator documents, in a fast library implementation. Every honest
  participant and observer runs it. This is a deliberate split: the coordinator is an authenticated,
  reproducible, tamper-evident bulletin board with structural gating; the cryptographic soundness of
  the whole transcript is established by the independent public verifier, which no one has to trust
  because anyone can run it.

## 4. How to contribute

You need a modern browser and an Internet Computer principal (an Internet Identity works).

1. Open the contributor page (`demo-frontend/contributor-client/`). Enter the coordinator canister
   id and host.
2. Sign in. Your principal is shown.
3. Click "Join the queue". You will see your position.
4. When it is your turn the page shows "YOUR TURN". Click "Contribute now". The page downloads the
   current parameters, samples and applies your secret in the browser, uploads the result, and
   submits the proof. Your secret is discarded when the transform finishes.
5. You can watch the transcript grow and later verify that your contribution is in it.

Everything privacy-critical happens in the wasm module `transform_contribution`, which returns only
public data. There is no export that carries the secret, so no network request the page makes can
carry it. You can confirm this by reading `demo-frontend/contributor-wasm/src/lib.rs`.

## 5. How to verify

Anyone can verify the finished ceremony without trusting the coordinator or any participant.

1. Download the published Phase-1 SRS and the published transcript (the coordinator serves the whole
   transcript; it can also be mirrored).
2. Build the standalone verifier from source:
   `cargo build --release --manifest-path ceremony/Cargo.toml --bin verify-transcript`.
3. Run it:
   `verify-transcript <phase1.srs.bin> <transcript.bin> --selfcheck`.

The verifier re-derives the initial parameters from the Phase-1 SRS and the exact circuits in this
repository, then replays every contribution: it checks each proof of knowledge, checks that every one
of the tens of thousands of parameter points was transformed by the same secret (the full
consistency check the coordinator leaves to off-chain), checks the transcript chaining and the beacon
finalize, and confirms the final keys prove and verify a real transfer and a real deposit. It shares
no code with the coordinator's own acceptance path, so it is a genuine independent check.

The verifier prints the final verifying-key SHA-256 for each circuit. The deployed ledger's verifying
keys must match these hashes. The coordinator wasm is reproducible (section 7), so you can also
confirm the deployed coordinator was built from this source.

## 6. Phase-1 decision

Requirement, measured from the circuits in this repository: the transfer circuit has 20146
constraints (QAP domain 2^15 = 32768) and the deposit circuit has 724 constraints (QAP domain
2^10 = 1024). Both specialize from one universal Phase-1 sized to the maximum, so Phase-1 must have
power at least 15.

Decision: inherit the Zcash Sapling BLS12-381 Powers of Tau (power 21, about 90 reviewed
participants). The alternatives were rejected: the Ethereum KZG ceremony is a KZG-structured string
that lacks the G2 powers and alpha and beta terms Groth16 needs, so it cannot produce Groth16
parameters; the Perpetual Powers of Tau and snarkjs `powersOfTau28_hez` are on the BN254 curve, not
BLS12-381; and running our own fresh Phase-1 would rest on a smaller participant set than the
reviewed Zcash setup and is discouraged by `docs/TRUSTED-SETUP-POLICY.md`. The Zcash Sapling powers
of tau is the reviewed, curve-correct, Groth16-shaped Phase-1 the policy calls for, and power 21
comfortably covers our required 15.

Ingestion is verified, never blind. The tool `ceremony-cli import-ptau` reads the pinned Sapling
response file, extracts the first 2^15 powers into the arkworks representation this repository uses,
runs the full pairing consistency check over the extracted string, and REFUSES to proceed on any
failure. It records the provenance (the upstream URL, the response-file SHA-256, the extracted-SRS
SHA-256, and the participant attestation set) into a `PROVENANCE.json`. A string is never consumed
without this structural check, so a wrong file or a wrong sub-format is rejected rather than trusted.

For local testing and the valueless demo, the same Phase-2 machinery runs against a locally generated
power-15 string that is structurally identical but carries a test-tier provenance label and is never
eligible for a real-value keyset. The Phase-2 code path is byte-identical for both; only the Phase-1
bytes differ, and swapping in the inherited string is the operator's step at launch.

## 7. Reproducible build

A ceremony coordinator whose binary cannot be reproduced is worthless, because its whole job is to be
trustlessly auditable. The coordinator wasm builds deterministically.

- One-command build: `coordinator/build.sh`. It writes `coordinator/coordinator.wasm` and
  `coordinator/BUILD-HASH.txt`.
- Verify a build: `coordinator/verify-build.sh` rebuilds from source and asserts the hash matches.
- Cross-machine: `coordinator/Dockerfile` pins the toolchain.
- Toolchain pins: dfx 0.31.0, moc 1.1.0, mops 2.8.0, mops packages core 1.0.0 and sha2 0.1.9. The
  build invokes the moc shipped with dfx, never a system `moc`.

Two clean builds on the pinned toolchain produce a byte-identical wasm. The published coordinator
wasm SHA-256 is recorded in `coordinator/BUILD-HASH.txt`. To confirm a deployed coordinator was built
from this source, compare the deployed module hash to that value, or run `verify-build.sh`.

The standalone verifier and the contributor client are Rust and wasm builds pinned by their
`Cargo.lock` files and the shared `ceremony` crate.

## 8. Honest limits

- The security argument is "1-of-N honest", not "trustless". If every contributor colludes and none
  destroys their secret, the beacon still prevents them from predicting the final parameters, but a
  ceremony with very few independent participants is weaker than one with many. Contribute if you can.
- The inherited Phase-1 rests on the Zcash Sapling participant set. We verify its structure and pin
  its hash, but its 1-of-N honesty is inherited, not re-run.
- On-chain acceptance verifies the proof of knowledge (soundness). The full parameter-consistency
  check is off-chain. A dishonest contributor cannot forge soundness on-chain, but could upload
  correctly-proven yet malformed parameters that break proving for later contributors. This is
  detected by the off-chain verifier and by the final key self-check, and the affected contribution
  can be excluded and the ceremony continued.
- This repository produces the coordinator, the client, the reproducible build, the verifier, and the
  spec, and proves them on a local replica. The mainnet deployment of the coordinator and the launch
  of the ceremony against the inherited Phase-1 string are the operator's steps and are not performed
  here.
